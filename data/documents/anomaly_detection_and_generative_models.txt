Anomaly Detection and Generative Models in Deep Learning
=========================================================

1. Anomaly Detection Overview
Anomaly detection (also called outlier detection or novelty detection) identifies data points that deviate significantly from expected patterns. Applications include:
- Manufacturing: Detecting defective products on production lines.
- Cybersecurity: Identifying intrusions or unusual network traffic.
- Healthcare: Detecting unusual medical images (tumors, lesions).
- Finance: Fraud detection in transactions.
- Industrial IoT: Predictive maintenance from sensor data.

Challenges:
- Rare anomalies mean highly imbalanced datasets.
- Anomalies are often undefined or unknown in advance.
- Most approaches use unsupervised or one-class learning.

2. Autoencoder-Based Anomaly Detection
Autoencoders are neural networks trained to reconstruct their input through a compressed bottleneck:
- Encoder: Compresses input to a low-dimensional latent representation.
- Bottleneck: Compact representation capturing essential features.
- Decoder: Reconstructs the original input from the latent representation.

For anomaly detection:
1. Train the autoencoder on normal data only.
2. The model learns to reconstruct normal patterns well.
3. At inference, compute reconstruction error.
4. High reconstruction error → likely anomaly.

Variants:
- Sparse Autoencoder: Constrains the latent representation to be sparse.
- Denoising Autoencoder: Trained to reconstruct clean input from noisy input.
- Variational Autoencoder (VAE): Learns a probabilistic latent space. Better for generation.
- Convolutional Autoencoder: Uses conv layers for image data.

3. Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)
VAEs add a probabilistic twist to autoencoders:
- The encoder outputs a distribution (mean μ and variance σ²) rather than a point.
- Sampling: z = μ + σ × ε, where ε ~ N(0,1) (reparameterization trick).
- Loss = Reconstruction loss + KL divergence (regularizes latent space to be Gaussian).

Benefits:
- Can generate new samples by sampling from the latent space.
- Smooth latent space allows interpolation between examples.
- Can be used for anomaly detection (low likelihood = anomaly).

4. Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)
GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014) consist of two competing networks:
- Generator (G): Generates fake samples from random noise.
- Discriminator (D): Classifies samples as real or fake.

Training: G tries to fool D; D tries to correctly classify. Min-max game:
min_G max_D V(D,G) = E[log D(x)] + E[log(1 - D(G(z)))]

GAN variants:
- DCGAN: Deep Convolutional GAN for high-quality images.
- Conditional GAN (cGAN): Generator conditioned on class labels.
- Pix2Pix: Image-to-image translation.
- CycleGAN: Unpaired image-to-image translation (e.g., horse to zebra).
- StyleGAN / StyleGAN2: High-resolution, photorealistic face generation.
- BigGAN: Large-scale class-conditional image synthesis.

GAN challenges:
- Mode collapse: Generator produces limited variety.
- Training instability: Difficult to balance G and D.
- Evaluation: FID (Fréchet Inception Distance) measures image quality and diversity.

5. Diffusion Models
Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020) are the current state-of-the-art in image generation:
- Forward process: Gradually add Gaussian noise to data over T timesteps.
- Reverse process: Train a neural network to predict and remove noise.
- At inference: Start from pure noise, iteratively denoise to generate a sample.

Notable models:
- DDPM (Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models): Original formulation.
- DDIM: Faster sampling, deterministic.
- Stable Diffusion: Latent diffusion in compressed space. Open-source.
- DALL-E 2/3 (OpenAI): Text-to-image generation.
- Imagen (Google): High-fidelity text-to-image.
- Midjourney: Commercial text-to-image tool.

Advantages over GANs:
- More stable training (no adversarial objective).
- Better mode coverage (no mode collapse).
- Competitive or superior image quality.

6. One-Class Classification
One-class classification trains only on normal data:
- One-Class SVM: Finds a hypersphere around normal data.
- Deep SVDD (Deep Support Vector Data Description): Neural network version.
- PatchCore: Memory-bank of normal feature patches. State-of-the-art for industrial anomaly detection.
- PADIM: Per-region modeling using pre-trained features.
- FastFlow: Normalizing flow-based anomaly detection.

7. Anomaly Detection Datasets
- MVTec AD: Industrial anomaly detection benchmark. 15 categories, 73 defect types.
- MNIST/CIFAR with one-class setup: Treat one class as normal, others as anomalies.
- KDD Cup 1999: Network intrusion detection.
- UCSD Pedestrian Dataset: Video anomaly detection.
- ShanghaiTech Campus: Complex scene video anomaly detection.

8. Normalizing Flows
Normalizing flows learn an invertible mapping between data and a simple distribution:
- Can compute exact likelihoods (unlike VAEs or GANs).
- Used for density estimation, anomaly detection, generative modeling.
- Examples: RealNVP, Glow, Flow++.

For anomaly detection: samples with low probability under the learned distribution are anomalies.

9. Metrics for Anomaly Detection
- AUROC (Area Under ROC Curve): Measures discrimination between normal and anomalous.
- AUPRC (Area Under Precision-Recall Curve): Better for imbalanced datasets.
- F1-Score: Balance between precision and recall at a threshold.
- Per-pixel AUROC: For localization of anomalies in images.
- PRO (Per-Region Overlap): Measures segmentation quality relative to defect size.

10. Synthesized Data for Training
When real anomaly data is scarce, synthesized anomalies can augment training:
- CutPaste: Cuts a patch from the image and pastes it elsewhere.
- Perlin noise textures: Simulate defect patterns.
- Geometric deformations: Simulate structural anomalies.
- DrAEM (Discriminatively trained Reconstruction-based Anomaly Detection): Uses synthetic anomalies.

Domain randomization: Vary textures, lighting, and background to improve generalization from synthetic to real data.
